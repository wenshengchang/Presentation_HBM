# Different Routes of Reaction Time Slowing in Cognitive Development and Aging

## Abstract

## Introduction

Simple reaction time (SRT) measures the minimum time required for a participant to initiate an overt response following the onset of a single, highly predictable stimulus (Donders, 1969; Johnson et al., 1985). The measure is one of the most replicated markers of cognitive change across the lifespan. SRT typically reduced from early through young adulthood, consistent with a global increase in the efficiency of neural information processing (Kail, 1991; Kail & Salthouse, 1994). From young adulthood onward, SRT gradually increases, reflecting a decline in processing speed that constrains performance across higher-order cognitive tasks (Salthouse, 1996, 2000). The resulting U-shaped trajectory has been replicated across task modalities, populations, and measurement platforms (Cerella, 1985; Cerella & Hale, 1994; Der & Deary, 2006; Fozard et al., 1994).

Large-scale studies confirm the robustness of this trajectory. Longitudinal data from over 1,200 adults (ages 17--96) show SRT slowing of about 0.5 ms per year beginning around age 20 (Fozard et al., 1994). A representative UK sample of over 7,000 adults found that RT slowing begins in the mid-20s and accelerates after age 60 (Der & Deary, 2006). Web-based data collection has extended these findings to samples exceeding 200,000 participants, confirming the curvilinear RT--age relationship and identifying education, health, and geographic factors as modifiers (Talboom et al., 2021; Woods et al., 2015). In addition, the slowing of SRT also accompanies with increments in intraindividual variability (Der & Deary, 2006; Dykiert et al., 2012; Fozard et al., 1994; Williams et al., 2005). The U-shaped behavioral pattern parallels with neurological development such as white matter myelination (Bartzokis et al., 2010; Chevalier et al., 2015). The consistency of the RT--age function across laboratory and remote platforms supports its status as a fundamental feature of cognitive development and aging.

Both the developmental and aging accounts share a core commitment: processing speed is unitary, changing in level but not in kind across the lifespan. The proportional slowing hypothesis formalizes this commitment. Older adults\' RTs across dozens of tasks can be predicted from young adults\' RTs by a multiplicative function, suggesting a single slowing factor (Cerella, 1985). An analogous proportional relationship holds between children\'s and young adults\' RTs (Cerella & Hale, 1994). This account treats all temporal components of the response as interchangeable. However, recent evidence shows that age-related RT slowing reflects slower response preparation rather than delayed initiation (Forrence et al., 2023). The delay between preparation and initiation remains constant across the adult lifespan. If the slowing factor were unitary, it should affect all temporal components proportionally. This dissociation suggests that the U-shaped RT trajectory is not the product of a single factor rising and falling, but a composite of separable processes that may follow distinct developmental schedules.

### Cognitive Factors Contributing to SRT Performance

Although SRT is often treated as a unitary measure, even this simplest of reaction time tasks engages a chain of functionally separable processing stages: sensory transduction of the stimulus, detection that a stimulus has appeared, temporal preparation for the response, motor programming, and initiation of the movement (Luce, 1986; Sanders, 1998). The additive factors method demonstrates that experimental manipulations can affect these stages independently (Sternberg, 1969; Sanders, 1998), supporting their functional separability. Each stage may follow a distinct developmental trajectory, and the contribution of each to the overall U-shaped RT function need not be uniform. Three classes of cognitive factors---temporal preparation, sustained attention, and motor programming---are particularly relevant because they differ in their developmental onset, neural substrate, and vulnerability to aging.

Among these, temporal preparation exerts the most pervasive influence on SRT. The measure is strongly modulated by the participant's state of readiness at the moment the stimulus appears. In variable-foreperiod paradigms, RT decreases as the foreperiod lengthens, reflecting the buildup of temporal expectancy (Niemi & Näätänen, 1981; Sanders, 1998). This foreperiod effect develops across childhood: four-year-olds show only arousal-driven sequential effects without the controlled monitoring component that produces the classic decreasing foreperiod--RT function, which does not emerge until approximately age five to six (Vallesi & Shallice, 2007). A dual-process account proposes that temporal preparation comprises both an automatic arousal component, available from early childhood, and a controlled component mediated by the right dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, which matures later (Vallesi, Shallice, & Walsh, 2007). In older adults, the foreperiod effect is generally preserved, but strategic, trial-to-trial adjustments in preparation are attenuated (Vallesi et al., 2009), suggesting that the flexible component of temporal preparation declines while automatic preparation remains intact. These age-related differences in temporal preparation contribute directly to both mean SRT and trial-to-trial variability.

Whereas temporal preparation shapes the baseline readiness for each trial, sustained attention determines how consistently that readiness is maintained across trials. Even within a brief SRT session, momentary fluctuations in sustained attention produce occasional slow responses that inflate the right tail of the RT distribution. Children exhibit greater RT variability than adults, and recent evidence suggests that this variability is specifically associated with lapses of sustained attention rather than with overall processing speed per se. In a population-based sample of over 1,000 children, inattention severity specifically predicted trial-to-trial RT variability, and this association held independently of mean speed (Aristodemou et al., 2024). The adaptive gain theory attributes these fluctuations to the locus coeruleus--norepinephrine (LC-NE) system, which regulates the balance between focused task engagement and disengaged exploration (Aston-Jones & Cohen, 2005). In older adults, attentional lapses also increase, and RT variability predicts subsequent cognitive decline beyond what mean RT predicts (Hultsch et al., 2002; Bunce et al., 2004). The critical difference is that children's lapses reflect an immature attentional control system---particularly prefrontal circuits supporting sustained attention---that has not yet reached adult efficiency, whereas older adults' lapses reflect degradation of a previously mature system. These attentional fluctuations are the primary source of distributional skewness in SRT.

The central processes of temporal preparation and sustained attention operate upstream of a peripheral motor chain that follows its own developmental schedule. SRT includes both pre-motor cognitive components and motor execution components, and these are partially dissociable. In children, motor execution speed improves dramatically from ages 4 to 12, reflecting musculoskeletal maturation and the development of motor coordination (Kiselev et al., 2009). In older adults, peripheral motor slowing is linked to reduced nerve conduction velocity and slowed muscle contraction (Woods et al., 2015). The separation of SRT from movement time (MT)---the interval between movement initiation and target contact---is therefore critical for isolating cognitive from biomechanical contributions to age-related slowing, a distinction that has been a foundational analytical goal in the chronometric tradition (Luce, 1986; Sanders, 1998).

### Beyond Mean RT: Distributional and Model-Based Approaches

The processing speed framework treats mean RT as a sufficient summary of performance. Under this assumption, trial-by-trial variability is measurement noise. Several lines of evidence challenge this view. Two individuals may produce identical mean RTs while differing in the shape of their RT distributions. One may respond near the mean on every trial; the other may produce a mixture of fast responses and occasional slow outliers. These distributional differences reflect variation in the cognitive processes that generate responses (Balota & Yap, 2011; Heathcote et al., 1991). The age groups that show the slowest mean RTs---young children and older adults---also show the most positively skewed distributions (West et al., 2002; Williams et al., 2005). This suggests that age-related slowing is driven by an increase in slow responses rather than a uniform shift in the entire distribution.

Intraindividual variability (IIV) across trials captures cognitive dimensions distinct from mean RT. Older adults exhibit greater IIV than younger adults, and this variability predicts subsequent cognitive decline beyond what mean RT predicts (Hultsch et al., 2002). A meta-analysis confirmed that older adults are more variable in both SRT and choice RT, and that this difference persists after controlling for mean RT level (Dykiert et al., 2012). In childhood, RT variability decreases from early childhood through adolescence, paralleling the decrease in mean RT but sometimes at a steeper rate (Williams et al., 2005). The neural basis of these changes is linked to white matter integrity: in adults, total white matter volume predicts RT instability after controlling for mean RT (Walhovd & Fjell, 2007), and in children, developmental reductions in IIV are associated with maturation of white matter microstructure (Tamnes et al., 2012). The spread and shape of the RT distribution carry information about the cognitive system that is independent of its average speed.

A related methodological concern is that total response time in reaching paradigms conflates pre-motor cognitive processes with motor execution. In a cross-sectional sample spanning childhood through older adulthood, where motor capabilities differ across age groups, this confound is not trivial. Separating the interval from stimulus onset to movement initiation (SRT) from the interval between movement initiation and target contact (movement time, MT) isolates stimulus detection and response preparation from reaching biomechanics. Distributional analysis of SRT, rather than total response time, is therefore the appropriate target for investigating cognitive components of processing speed.

An SRT trial engages a chain of processing stages that differ in their susceptibility to developmental change. Sensory transduction and motor initiation are constrained by peripheral neural and musculoskeletal factors that mature in childhood and decline in old age, following the trajectory of white matter myelination (Bartzokis et al., 2010). Detection and response preparation depend on central processes---alertness, sustained attention, and the efficiency of stimulus--response mapping---that follow their own developmental trajectories (Sanders, 1998). Between-person differences in SRT may reflect stable features of this processing chain: one person may have a faster peripheral system, another a more efficient central system. Within-person variability across trials reflects the consistency with which these stages operate from moment to moment (Hultsch et al., 2002; Walhovd & Fjell, 2007). A parametric decomposition of the SRT distribution can, in principle, separate these sources. A model whose parameters map onto distinct stages---peripheral latency, central processing speed, trial-to-trial consistency---would allow us to ask whether age-related slowing in children and older adults arises from the same or different components of this chain.

Several parametric families have been used to decompose RT distributions. The ex-Gaussian distribution, a convolution of Gaussian and exponential components, is the most widely applied (Heathcote et al., 1991; Hockley, 1984; Luce, 1986). Its exponential tail component (τ) is often the most sensitive to experimental manipulations and individual differences (Balota & Yap, 2011), and older adults' RT slowing in the Stroop task is attributable primarily to increases in τ, with modest changes in the Gaussian mean (Spieler et al., 1996). The ex-Gaussian has proven useful across a range of paradigms and populations, including developmental and clinical samples (Hwang-Gu et al., 2013; Leth-Steensen et al., 2000). The log-normal distribution is another option with practical advantages for modeling multiplicative effects (Lindeløv et al., 2021), though it is often empirically indistinguishable from the ex-Gaussian (Cousineau, 2004).

The present study adopts the shifted Weibull distribution, following the SRT and hierarchical Bayesian modeling literature established by Rouder and colleagues (Rouder et al., 2003, 2005). Like the ex-Gaussian, the Weibull provides a three-parameter characterization of positively skewed RT distributions. Its parameters---shift (*t*₀), scale (λ), and shape (κ)---have been given a stage-based interpretation: the shift represents the minimum latency below which no response can occur, indexing peripheral processes (Dzhafarov, 1992; Luce, 1986); the scale indexes the spread of the distribution above this minimum; and the shape governs distributional skewness and the consistency of processing across trials. The Weibull arises from race models of RT (Colonius, 1995; Logan, 1988, 1992), providing a theoretical link between distributional parameters and latent cognitive mechanisms. Empirical selective influence evidence supports the distinctness of the parameters: in a symbolic distance task, stimulus difficulty affected the scale parameter without altering the shift (Rouder et al., 2005). The primary reason for adopting the Weibull in the present study is pragmatic: Rouder et al. (2003, 2005) developed a hierarchical Bayesian framework specifically for the Weibull that is designed to provide stable estimates with small numbers of observations per participant---a critical advantage given the brief protocols typical of developmental assessment.

How does the distributional approach relate to the drift-diffusion model (DDM)? The DDM is a process model: it specifies a mechanism of evidence accumulation and derives RT distributions from that mechanism (Ratcliff, 1978; Ratcliff & McKoon, 2008). This confers interpretive power---drift rate, boundary separation, and nondecision time map onto specific cognitive operations---but requires two-choice tasks with both correct and error responses to identify its parameters. DDM analyses have provided the most direct evidence that children and older adults are slow for qualitatively different reasons: children show lower drift rates, wider boundaries, and longer nondecision times, whereas older adults show comparable drift rates but more conservative decision criteria and substantially longer nondecisional components (Ratcliff et al., 2006, 2012; Theisen et al., 2021). SRT tasks, however, produce only correct detections and lack the error structure that constrains the DDM. The Weibull decomposition makes fewer mechanistic commitments than the DDM while still capturing key distributional features, and it can be applied to SRT where the DDM cannot. This makes it suited for a study of SRT where the primary goal is to characterize *how* distributional properties change with age, before investigating *why* they change.

The same decomposition can be applied to MT. Because MT reflects ballistic execution rather than stimulus-driven decision processes, its parameters should show a different profile. The shift should carry a larger share of age-related variance, reflecting biomechanical limits of the effector system. The shape should be less sensitive to attentional factors that govern trial-to-trial consistency in SRT. If the SRT and MT parameters show weak cross-measure correlations, this confirms that the decomposition captures distinct processes in the two response phases.

### Hierarchical Bayesian Estimation and Scalable Assessment

App-based and tablet-based assessment platforms have made large-scale RT data collection feasible across the developmental and aging range. Tablet-based cognitive tasks produce data comparable to laboratory administration while allowing efficient group testing in schools, community centers, and homes (Bignardi et al., 2021). Touchscreen devices record movement time separately from reaction time at the hardware level, providing the SRT/MT decomposition without specialized equipment. However, app-based tasks typically yield fewer trials per participant than laboratory protocols. Sessions must be kept short for young children and older adults, and outlier rates are higher in these groups. The result is a characteristic data structure: many participants, each contributing a modest number of usable trials.

This data structure creates a tension with distributional analysis. Maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) of distribution parameters at the individual level is unstable when trial counts are small (Rouder et al., 2003). Hierarchical Bayesian modeling (HBM) resolves this tension by exploiting exactly the feature that app-based data provides---a large sample. HBM treats individual-level parameters as draws from group-level distributions (Rouder et al., 2005). This partial pooling shrinks extreme individual estimates toward the group mean, borrowing strength across the many participants to stabilize each person's estimates. The advantage over MLE is greatest when trial counts are small and between-person variability is high---precisely the conditions that characterize developmental samples collected via app-based platforms.

HBM also provides a natural framework for propagating uncertainty. Rather than producing point estimates of distributional parameters, HBM returns full posterior distributions that reflect estimation uncertainty at both the individual and group levels. Model fit can be evaluated through posterior predictive checks, which compare observed data features to the distribution of those features implied by the fitted model (Gelman et al., 2013). This approach is preferable to relying on summary fit indices that may mask systematic misfit in the tails of the distribution---where age-related differences are often largest.

### The Present Study

We fitted shifted Weibull distributions via hierarchical Bayesian modeling to SRT data from 1,652 participants across five age groups: children aged 4--6 (*n* = 108), 7--12 (*n* = 117), and 13--17 (*n* = 56), young adults aged 18--30 (*n* = 1,299), and older adults aged 65 and above (*n* = 72). MT was analyzed in parallel as a methodological control. The large young adult sample serves as a stable reference for the mature perceptual-motor system. The design targets the developmental and aging limbs of the RT--age function rather than providing continuous coverage of the middle decades. Three research questions guided the analyses. First, do the three Weibull parameters form a coherent latent factor consistent with unitary processing speed, or do they remain empirically distinct? Second, do children and older adults show the same or different patterns of parametric deviation from young adults---and if different, which parameters carry the most age-related variance for SRT versus MT? Third, does the correlation structure among parameters change across age groups, as predicted by the dedifferentiation hypothesis (Baltes & Lindenberger, 1997; Li et al., 2004)?

## Methods

### Participants

A total of 1,815 individuals were recruited from community and school settings, spanning ages 4 to over 80 years. Middle-aged adults (31--64 years) were underrepresented due to scheduling constraints of community-based daytime recruitment and are not included in the present analyses. Each participant completed a visuomotor reaching task comprising 20 trials, from which simple reaction time (SRT) and movement time (MT) were recorded. Following data screening (see Data Preparation), 1,652 participants were retained for analysis.

Participants were stratified into five age cohorts: early childhood (\<7 years, n = 110), middle childhood (7--12 years, n = 117), adolescence (13--17 years, n = 56), young adulthood (18--30 years, n = 1,297), and older adulthood (over 65 years, n = 72). The young adult group served as a normative reference representing the mature state of perceptual-motor function. Unequal group sizes reflect naturalistic recruitment and are accommodated through Welch-corrected inferential tests and Games--Howell post-hoc comparisons (see Statistical Analysis).

All participants had normal or corrected-to-normal vision and reported no history of neurological or psychiatric disorders. The study protocol was approved by the Social and Behavioral Research Ethical Principles and Regulations of National Taiwan University, Taiwan (202305EM073, 202409EM029). Written informed consent was obtained from all adult participants; for minors, a parent or legal guardian provided written consent and the participant provided verbal assent.

### Task and Procedure

Participants performed a tablet-based visuomotor reaching task designed to dissociate motor initiation from motor execution (Figure 1). The task followed an SRT paradigm with an added reaching component, enabling the extraction of two distinct temporal measures from each trial.

Participants maintained continuous contact with a designated circle using their non-dominant index finger throughout the session; the task paused automatically if this contact was released. Each trial began when the participant pressed and held a red start square (5 × 5 cm) at the bottom of the screen with the dominant index finger. After a variable foreperiod of 1,000--3,000 ms, a cyan target square appeared at a fixed diagonal location. The target did not appear unless continuous contact with the start square was maintained, preventing anticipatory releases. Participants were instructed to release the start square and touch the target as quickly and accurately as possible. Each participant completed 5 practice trials followed by 20 recorded trials. SRT was defined as the interval from target onset to release of the start square, and MT as the interval from release of the start square to contact with the target.

### Data Preparation

Outlier detection was conducted using a log-transformed version of Tukey\'s fences. Because RT distributions are typically right-skewed, all SRT and MT values were first log-transformed to approximate normality. The interquartile range (IQR) was computed on the log-transformed data, with outliers defined as values falling outside the fences $\lbrack Q_{1} - 2*IQR,\ Q_{3} + 2*IQR\rbrack$. A trial was excluded if either its SRT or MT was identified as an outlier. Trials with SRT below 150 ms were additionally removed to eliminate anticipatory responses. Participants retaining fewer than 16 valid trials after screening were excluded from all subsequent analyses.

### The Shifted Weibull Model

To characterize the underlying distributions of both SRT and MT, we modeled the trial-level data using a three-parameter shifted Weibull distribution (Logan, 1992; Rouder et al., 2005). For an observed RT value $x$, the probability density function is defined as:

$$f\left( x;\ \theta \right) = \frac{\kappa}{\lambda}\left( \frac{x - t_{0}}{\lambda} \right)^{\kappa - 1}e^{- \left( \frac{x - t_{0}}{\lambda} \right)^{\kappa}},\ \ \ \ x > t_{0}.$$

where parameters $\theta = \left( t_{0},\ \lambda,\ \kappa \right)$. The shift parameter ($t_{0}$) represents the leading edge of the distribution i.e., the minimum possible reaction time. The scale parameter ($\lambda$) the spread of the distribution beyond $t_{0}$, with larger values indicating greater dispersion of RTs across trials. The shape parameter ($\kappa$) controls the form of the distribution. When $\kappa < 1$ the hazard function is decreasing, when $\kappa = 1$ the distribution reduces to a shifted exponential, and when $\kappa > 1$ the hazard function is increasing, producing a more peaked and symmetric distribution.

### Hierarchical Bayes Modeling (HBM)

Individual RT distributions were estimated within a hierarchical Bayesian framework (HBM; Rouder et al., 2003, 2005). The framework consists of individual-level Weibull parameters which determine the RT distribution, with individual parameters sampled from underlying parent distributions described by group-level hyperparameters. The two levels of parameters are jointly estimated, allowing information to be shared across participants within age groups. For participant *i* in age group *g* and trial *j*, RT was modeled as

$${RT}_{ij} = t_{0i} + Weibull\ \left( \lambda_{i},\ \kappa_{i} \right),$$

where $t_{0i}$≥ 0 is the shift parameter, $\lambda_{i}$\> 0 is the scale parameter, and $\kappa_{i}$ \> 0 is the shape parameter. Individual parameters were drawn from age-group-specific parent distributions, allowing information to be shared across participants within each cohort. The posterior distribution of individual parameters was obtained by combining the likelihood of observed data with hierarchical priors:

$$p\left( t_{0i},\lambda_{i},\ \kappa_{i} \middle| {RT}_{i \bullet},\Phi\lbrack g\rbrack \right) \propto \ p\left( {RT}_{i \bullet} \middle| t_{0i},\lambda_{i},\ \kappa_{i} \right)*p\left( t_{0i},\lambda_{i},\ \kappa_{i} \middle| \Phi\lbrack g\rbrack \right),$$

where ${RT}_{i \bullet}$ denotes all observed trials for participant *i* and $\Phi\lbrack g\rbrack$ represents group-level hyperparameters. Hierarchical priors were specified as: t₀ᵢ \~ Uniform(0, min(RTᵢ.)), λᵢ⁻¹ \~ Gamma(ξ₁\[g\], ξ₂\[g\]), and κᵢ \~ Gamma(η₁\[g\], η₂\[g\]), where group-level hyperparameters (ξ₁, ξ₂, η₁, η₂) were estimated from data with weakly informative hyperpriors (cf*.* Rouder et al., 2003). This hierarchical structure induces adaptive shrinkage. Individual parameter estimates are pulled toward the group mean, with the degree of shrinkage depending on the relative precision of each participant\'s data versus the group-level distribution. Participants with fewer trials or more variable data are shrunk more strongly toward the group estimate, while those with more reliable data retain more individual information.

To address the highly unequal group sizes in the present sample, hyperprior variances were scaled by group size to prevent excessive shrinkage in larger groups while maintaining appropriate regularization in smaller groups. Posterior distributions were sampled using Gibbs sampling with Metropolis--Hastings steps for non-conjugate updates (Carlin et al., 1992; Carter & Kohn, 1994). Four independent chains were run with overdispersed starting values, each for 60,000 iterations with a 20,000-iteration burn-in. Every 5th sample was retained (thinning factor = 5), yielding 32,000 posterior samples (8,000 per chain) per parameter. Individual parameter estimates were summarized as posterior medians, with uncertainty quantified by 95% highest density intervals. Full details of the sampling procedure and optimization strategies are provided in the Supplementary Materials.

### Model Evaluation

Convergence was assessed using the Gelman--Rubin R̂ statistic , with R̂ \< 1.10 as the criterion (Gelman et al., 2013), and effective sample size (ESS), with ESS \> 100 as the minimum threshold for reliable posterior inference (Kruschke, 2014). Both diagnostics were computed for every individual-level parameter across the four chains.

Goodness of fit was evaluated using four complementary metrics, applied separately to SRT and MT. First, root mean squared error (RMSE) between 19 model-implied quantiles (5th--95th percentile) and the corresponding empirical quantiles provided a scale-interpretable measure of distributional misfit in milliseconds. Second, mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) normalized this discrepancy by RT magnitude, enabling comparison across age groups with substantially different absolute RTs. Third, 90% prediction interval coverage assessed calibration of the model\'s posterior uncertainty (nominal target: .90). Fourth, posterior predictive p-values (Gelman et al., 2013), computed from 200 replicated datasets, targeted two summary statistics: the minimum RT (testing shift parameter calibration) and the standard deviation (testing whether the model captures trial-to-trial variability). Values near .50 indicate adequate fit; values below .05 or above .95 indicate systematic misfit. Detailed diagnostics by parameter and age group are reported in the Supplementary Materials.

### Analytic Strategy for Parameter-Level Inference

Following model validation, age-group differences were examined for each of the six individual-level Weibull parameter estimates through univariate comparisons. Because this approach treats the parameters as carrying distinct information, we first assessed the empirical justification for this assumption. Pairwise Pearson correlations were computed across the full sample (*N* = 1,652), along with partial correlations controlling for continuous age. The partial correlation matrix served as the primary basis for evaluation, as pooling across age groups with different mean levels inflates bivariate associations through shared developmental trends. Homogeneity of within-group covariance matrices was assessed with Box\'s M statistic (Box, 1949), though its sensitivity to large and unequal samples is well documented and was considered in interpretation (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2013). If partial correlations among the parameters were modest, indicating that they capture largely non-overlapping sources of individual differences, separate univariate comparisons would be warranted. Strong correlations would instead call for a multivariate approach or dimensionality reduction. Effect sizes for correlations followed Cohen (1988): *r* \< .10 negligible, .10--.29 small, .30--.49 moderate, ≥ .50 large.

Each parameter was then compared across the five age cohorts using Welch\'s ANOVA (Welch, 1951), which does not assume homogeneity of variance and is appropriate given the substantial group size imbalance (*n* = 56 to 1,297). Effect sizes were quantified with eta-squared (η²), interpreted as small (.01), medium (.06), and large (.14) following Cohen (1988). Kruskal--Wallis *H* tests were computed as nonparametric robustness checks. Where omnibus tests reached significance, pairwise comparisons used the Games--Howell procedure (Games & Howell, 1976), which accommodates unequal variances and sample sizes. Standardized mean differences (Cohen\'s *d*) were computed for each pair. A significance threshold of α = .05 was applied throughout. Because the six parameters index theoretically distinct aspects of the RT distribution, no familywise correction was applied across parameters (Rothman, 1990). We interpret the overall pattern of comparisons rather than individual isolated tests.

The pattern of age-group differences was evaluated against two theoretical benchmarks. Under the proportional slowing hypothesis, all three parameters within a given task should follow the same developmental ordering across groups. Departures from this pattern would indicate that age-related slowing is not unitary. Additionally, if children and older adults deviate from young adults on different parameters, this would provide evidence for dissociable sources of RT prolongation, extending findings from choice RT decomposition (Ratcliff et al., 2012) to the simple RT domain.

## Results

### Summary Statistics

Participants retained around 18 valid trials across all ages after outlier removal (Table 1). None of the 1,652 participants was excluded due to insufficient valid SRT trials. The young (\< 7 years), middle (7--12 years), adolescent (13--17 years), adult (18--30 years), and older adult (\> 65 years) groups included 108 (65 males, 43 females), 117 (94 males, 23 females), 56 (50 males, 6 females), 1,299 (826 males, 473 females), and 72 (27 males, 45 females) participants, respectively. Both SRT and MT showed age-dependent decreases from early childhood through young adulthood, followed by increases in the oldest group, reflecting the expected U-shaped trajectory of processing speed and motor control (Figure 2). RT variability stabilized in adolescence and converged toward adult levels, indicating increased consistency in performance.

### HBM Results

Convergence was satisfactory for both models (Figure 3). For SRT, 95.7% of all individual-level parameters met the R̂ \< 1.10 criterion, with convergence rates of 95.2% (t₀), 96.7% (λ), and 95.1% (κ). Median R̂ values were near unity (1.002--1.003 across parameters). The median ESS ranged from 604 (t₀) to 884 (κ). For MT, overall convergence was 98.5% with parameter-specific rates of 97.8% (t₀), 99.4% (λ), and 98.4% (κ). The median ESS ranged from 413 (t₀) to 633 (κ). The youngest children (\< 7 years) showed the lowest convergence rate for SRT (Group 1: 99.7% for SRT overall) but the lowest MT convergence was in the oldest group (92.6%), consistent with greater heterogeneity in motor execution at the extremes of the lifespan. Parameters that did not converge were retained to avoid selection bias; their posterior medians were stable across chains despite elevated R̂ (see Supplementary Materials).

Goodness-of-fit metrics supported the adequacy of the shifted Weibull model across all age groups (Table 2). For SRT, quantile-level RMSE ranged from 12.4 ± 9.7 ms (13--17 years) to 78.9 ± 129.1 ms (\< 7 years). For MT, RMSE ranged from 16.5 ± 53.5 ms (adults) to 116.8 ± 163.2 ms (\< 7 years). Overall Pearson correlations among the six parameters were generally moderate to strong, reflecting in part the shared developmental trends (Figure 4, Panel A). ## Discussions

### Summary of Main Findings

Hierarchical Bayesian estimation of shifted Weibull distributions decomposed simple reaction time (SRT) and movement time (MT) into three distributional parameters---shift (*t*₀), scale (λ), and shape (κ)---across five age groups spanning childhood through older adulthood. The Weibull parameters did not behave as a unitary processing speed factor: children and older adults deviated from young adults through qualitatively different parametric profiles. For SRT, the scale parameter (λ) carried the most age-related variance (η² = .478), consistent with the interpretation that developmental and aging differences in SRT are driven primarily by central processing efficiency rather than by the sensorimotor floor, while the shift parameter (*t*₀) also contributed substantially (η² = .289). For MT, the pattern shifted: *t*₀ dominated (η² = .375), consistent with peripheral motor constraints, closely followed by λ (η² = .358). Critically, the youngest children (\< 7 years) and older adults (65+) showed comparable *t*₀ elevations relative to young adults (*d* = 1.47 and −1.38, respectively) but diverged sharply in how the other parameters contributed---most notably in the shape parameter, where children showed significantly reduced κ relative to both adults and older adults, while older adults' κ was fully preserved. This dissociation indicates that the U-shaped mean RT trajectory is a composite of distinct processing changes at the developmental and aging limbs, rather than the product of a single slowing factor that rises and falls.

### Dissociable Routes of Slowing: Children Are Not Small Elderly

The most consequential finding is that children and older adults arrive at similarly slow SRT means via distinguishable distributional mechanisms. For SRT, the youngest children showed large deviations from young adults across all three parameters: elevated λ (*d* = 1.65), elevated *t*₀ (*d* = 1.47), and reduced κ (*d* = −0.83). The λ and κ effects implicate central processing: children were both slower in their typical processing speed and more inconsistent from trial to trial, producing heavily right-skewed distributions with a long tail of slow responses. This profile is consistent with the temporal preparation and sustained attention literatures, which show that young children have difficulty maintaining an optimal level of readiness over variable foreperiods (Vallesi & Shallice, 2007) and exhibit greater attentional fluctuation during repetitive tasks (Hultsch et al., 2002). The reduced κ, in particular, suggests that children's slowing is not merely a matter of slower processing on every trial but reflects an increased proportion of trials on which processing is substantially delayed---a pattern compatible with intermittent lapses in attentional engagement rather than a stable deficit in processing speed.

Older adults (65+) showed a partially overlapping but distinguishable parametric profile. Their largest SRT effect was in *t*₀ (*d* = −1.38), consistent with a substantial elevation of the irreducible sensorimotor floor under the stage-based interpretation (Rouder et al., 2005). This is consistent with Forrence et al. (2023), who demonstrated that age-related SRT slowing in adults reflects delayed response preparation rather than delayed initiation, and with the neurobiological evidence that white matter degradation in older adulthood increases peripheral conduction times (Bartzokis et al., 2010; Salthouse, 1996). A potential confound is that older adults may be less familiar with touchscreen devices than the predominantly university-recruited young adults. However, the SRT response in this task is a finger *lift* from a button already in contact---a minimal motor act that does not require touchscreen navigation skill---making tablet unfamiliarity a less plausible explanation for the SRT *t*₀ elevation than for the MT *t*₀ elevation, where the reaching movement to a target is more device-dependent. Furthermore, the elderly *t*₀ elevation is consistent in direction and magnitude with laboratory-based studies using standard button-press apparatus (Der & Deary, 2006; Fozard et al., 1994), suggesting that the effect reflects genuine sensorimotor slowing rather than device unfamiliarity alone. We also note that as discussed in the Introduction, part of the elderly *t*₀ elevation may reflect increased response caution---a strategic shift toward prioritizing accuracy over speed---rather than purely peripheral slowing. The present SRT task cannot distinguish these contributions, as both would inflate *t*₀; future work combining SRT with explicit speed--accuracy instructions could separate the strategic from the sensorimotor component. Older adults also showed a large λ effect (*d* = −1.16), suggesting that their processing speed above the sensorimotor floor was also substantially reduced under the interpretation of λ as central processing efficiency. The critical distinction lies in the shape parameter: older adults' κ did not differ significantly from young adults (*d* = 0.14, *p* = 1.00), indicating fully preserved trial-to-trial processing consistency. Moreover, children and older adults differed significantly on κ_SRT (*d* = −0.65, *p* \< .001), confirming that the shape parameter cleanly differentiates the two groups. The elderly profile is thus characterized as a *compound* slowing---elevated floor *plus* reduced efficiency---with fully preserved processing regularity, rather than pure peripheral decline. The children's profile, by contrast, adds a third dimension of impairment: reduced consistency, reflecting an increased proportion of trials on which processing is substantially delayed.

This dissociation extends findings from the drift-diffusion model (DDM) literature, where Ratcliff et al. (2006, 2010) showed that older adults' choice RT slowing reflects increased boundary separation (response caution) and elevated non-decision time, while children's slowing reflects reduced drift rates and greater cross-trial variability in non-decision time (Ratcliff et al., 2012). The present results demonstrate that an analogous decomposition holds for SRT, a task where the DDM is not identifiable because it produces only correct detections without the error structure needed to constrain drift rate and boundary separation. The Weibull decomposition provides a complementary parametric lens: under the stage-based interpretation, the shift parameter corresponds to what the DDM subsumes under non-decision time, the scale parameter indexes processing efficiency analogous to (though not identical with) drift rate, and the shape parameter indexes a dimension of trial-to-trial consistency that DDM distributes across its variability parameters. That the children--elderly dissociation is visible even when the decision component is removed (SRT has no stimulus discrimination or response selection) suggests that the dissociation resides in what DDM would lump together as non-decision time---specifically, in the subcomponents of encoding, temporal preparation, and motor programming that Weibull parameters can separate.

The SRT--MT contrast serves as internal validation of this parametric decomposition. For SRT, the dominant parameter was λ (η² = .478 vs. .289 for *t*₀), consistent with the view that age-group differences in stimulus-driven initiation are primarily driven by central processes. For MT, the dominant parameter was *t*₀ (η² = .375 vs. .358 for λ), consistent with the view that age-group differences in motor execution are primarily driven by peripheral and biomechanical constraints. The shape parameter (κ) showed a larger effect for MT (η² = .149) than for SRT (η² = .085), suggesting that the consistency of motor execution is more developmentally sensitive than the consistency of response initiation---plausibly because motor execution involves biomechanical degrees of freedom (limb coordination, force calibration) that are still maturing in children and declining in older adults, whereas response initiation is a ballistic trigger with fewer sources of trial-to-trial variation. This crossing pattern across response phases is incompatible with a single slowing factor and supports the view that the Weibull parameters index functionally distinct processing stages (Rouder et al., 2005).

### Correlation Structure and the Dedifferentiation Hypothesis

The third research question concerned whether the correlation structure among Weibull parameters changes across age groups, as predicted by the dedifferentiation hypothesis (Baltes & Lindenberger, 1997; Li et al., 2004). Box's *M* test confirmed significant heterogeneity of within-group covariance matrices (*M* = 7855.86, *p* \< .001), though the extreme denominator degrees of freedom and the test's known sensitivity to large samples (Tabachnick & Fidell, 2013) limit interpretive weight.

Inspection of the pooled within-group correlation matrix, and the within-group matrices individually, revealed a pattern that partially contradicts the dedifferentiation prediction. The three middle age groups (7--12, 13--17, 18--30) showed broadly similar correlation structures, consistent with a stable factorial organization of distributional parameters across the period of rapid cognitive development and into maturity. The youngest children (\< 7) showed stronger negative *t*₀--κ associations than other groups, suggesting that in early childhood, the peripheral floor and processing consistency are more tightly coupled---when the irreducible latency is high, the variable component of processing is compressed into a narrower window, producing less symmetric distributions. Older adults, contrary to the dedifferentiation prediction, showed weaker cross-parameter associations rather than stronger ones. If dedifferentiation extends to the subcomponent level within a single RT distribution, the parameters should become more intercorrelated as biological constraints increasingly limit performance across processing stages in old age. The data did not support this.

Two interpretations merit consideration. First, dedifferentiation may operate at a different grain size than the one tested here. The hypothesis was formulated primarily at the psychometric level---across broad cognitive abilities measured by diverse tasks (fluid intelligence, memory, processing speed; Tucker-Drob, 2009; Hartung et al., 2018). The parameters of a Weibull distribution fitted to a single SRT task may index processes that are too fine-grained, and too constrained by task physics, to show the kind of general biological coupling that dedifferentiation envisions. Testing dedifferentiation at the level of distributional parameters within a single task is novel---most prior tests use across-task psychometric batteries---and our null result may represent a boundary condition rather than a disconfirmation. Second, the modest elderly sample (*n* = 72) may have been insufficiently powered to detect subtle changes in correlation structure, particularly given the high within-group variability that characterizes this age group. Future studies with larger elderly samples, and ideally with longitudinal follow-up, will be needed to adjudicate.

A related question is whether the six Weibull parameters should be analyzed individually or reduced to latent composites via factor analysis. The correlation data provide a clear answer without requiring formal factor extraction. The overall correlation matrix showed moderate-to-strong associations among the parameters (e.g., λ~SRT~--λ~MT~ *r* = .66; *t*₀~SRT~--*t*₀~MT~ *r* = .50), which might suggest a common latent factor. However, the pooled within-group matrix---which removes the between-group mean differences that inflate these associations---told a dramatically different story. Cross-parameter correlations collapsed: λ~SRT~--λ~MT~ dropped from .66 to .22 (a 67% reduction), *t*₀~SRT~--λ~SRT~ dropped from .42 to .07 (83% reduction), and κ~SRT~--κ~MT~ dropped from .18 to .08 (58% reduction). The apparent latent structure in the overall matrix was almost entirely a developmental confound---children and older adults produce correlated parameter values across tasks because their processing systems are at the same developmental stage, not because a stable individual-differences factor links these parameters. Furthermore, Box's *M* test confirmed that the covariance structure differs fundamentally across age groups (*M* = 7855.86, *p* \< .001), violating a core assumption of pooled factor analysis. Given that the adult group constitutes 78.5% of the total sample, a pooled EFA would effectively recover the adult structure while the developmental groups---precisely those of theoretical interest---would have negligible influence on the solution. Within age groups, most cross-parameter associations fall below *r* = .25, well below conventional thresholds for meaningful factor loading. The one notable exception---the *t*₀--κ association within SRT (*r*~pooled~ = −.48)---reflects a within-task speed--consistency tradeoff, not a cross-task latent factor, and may partly reflect a statistical dependency in Weibull parameter estimation where shift and shape can trade off during fitting. Together, these observations support the present analytic strategy of treating the six parameters as empirically distinct individual-differences dimensions, and they argue against reducing them to composites that would discard the very parametric specificity that motivates the Weibull approach.

### The Shifted Weibull as a Distributional Tool for SRT

The choice of the shifted Weibull over the ex-Gaussian or other distributional families was motivated primarily by the availability of Rouder et al.'s (2003, 2005) hierarchical Bayesian framework, which was designed specifically for the Weibull and provides stable parameter estimates with small trial counts. The ex-Gaussian remains a viable alternative and has been applied successfully in developmental and clinical RT research (Hwang-Gu et al., 2013; Leth-Steensen et al., 2000; Spieler et al., 1996). The Weibull's stage-based interpretation---*t*₀ as the peripheral floor, λ as the spread of processing above this floor, κ as processing consistency---offered a useful framework for generating predictions about which distributional components should differ between SRT and MT and across age groups, but we do not claim that the Weibull is inherently superior to the ex-Gaussian for all purposes.

The present data provide new selective influence evidence for the Weibull decomposition, though the epistemic status of this evidence requires careful specification. The cognitive interpretations of Weibull parameters---*t*₀ as peripheral latency, λ as central efficiency, κ as processing consistency---were motivated by prior theoretical work (Rouder et al., 2005; Dzhafarov, 1992) and experimental evidence from symbolic distance tasks, where stimulus difficulty selectively affected the scale parameter without altering the shift (Rouder et al., 2005). Using the present age-group data to both validate and apply these mappings would risk circularity. The critical non-circular evidence is the SRT--MT contrast, which does not depend on the age comparison: for SRT (a centrally-driven task), λ dominated age-related variance (η² = .478); for MT (a peripherally-driven task), *t*₀ dominated (η² = .375). This crossing interaction is predicted by the stage-based account and would not occur if the three parameters were interchangeable reflections of a single speed factor. The age comparisons are *consistent* with the parameter interpretations but are not the sole basis for them. The within-group pooled correlations provide additional non-circular evidence: *t*₀~SRT~ and λ~SRT~ were nearly independent (*r*~pooled~ = .07), and κ~SRT~ and κ~MT~ were only weakly associated (*r*~pooled~ = .08), confirming that same-parameter estimates across response phases index partly independent individual-differences dimensions. These results extend the selective influence evidence from the experimental domain to the developmental domain, where age acts as a naturally occurring source of influence on distinct processing stages---but we acknowledge that a definitive within-study test would require experimental manipulation of foreperiod variability, stimulus intensity, or cognitive load alongside the developmental comparison. Myerson and Lawrence (2007) also applied the Weibull to aging RT distributions, but in choice RT paradigms; the present study demonstrates the utility of the Weibull decomposition for simple RT, where the absence of a decision component simplifies the mapping between parameters and processing stages.

### Methodological Contributions

Three methodological features of this study merit discussion as contributions to the RT distributional analysis literature. First, the hierarchical Bayesian framework proved essential for the data structure at hand. With only 20 formal trials per participant, individual-level maximum likelihood estimation of three-parameter Weibull distributions would be unstable and frequently yield boundary estimates (Rouder et al., 2003). The HBM's partial pooling stabilized individual estimates by borrowing strength across participants within each age group. Critically, the group-level hyperparameters that govern this pooling are not estimated from any single participant's 20 trials but from the full group (*n* = 56 to 1,299), giving them a precision that does not depend on within-subject trial counts. Rouder et al. (2005) demonstrated via simulation that this hierarchical Weibull model recovers individual parameters more accurately than MLE, Vincentizing, or quantile-based estimation specifically when trial counts are low (25 trials per participant in their simulations, comparable to the present protocol). The shrinkage is adaptive: participants with fewer usable trials or more extreme data are pulled more strongly toward their group mean, while those with many consistent trials retain estimates close to their individual MLEs. This produces conservative estimates of between-person variability---shrinkage attenuates individual extremes, which would, if anything, understate rather than overstate the age-group differences reported here. The convergence diagnostics (95.7% for SRT and 98.5% for MT, median ESS of 713 and 492, respectively) and posterior predictive checks (coverage .94--.95) confirm that the model's uncertainty quantification is well calibrated, not that estimation uncertainty is hidden. We emphasize that the present conclusions rest on group-level parameter comparisons (ANOVA, post-hoc contrasts), which benefit from the full sample size, not on individual-level diagnosis. The lower ESS for MT (median 126 vs. 177 for SRT) in the adult group suggests that the large group size introduced greater posterior complexity, and future implementations might benefit from longer chains or more aggressive thinning for very large groups.

Second, the tablet-based assessment platform demonstrated that distributional analysis of SRT is feasible outside the laboratory. The touchscreen device provided hardware-level separation of SRT from MT, yielding the SRT/MT decomposition without specialized response boxes. Mean trial retention was high across age groups (17.8--18.6 out of 20 trials), and outlier rates were modest (6.8--10.9%). The practical implication is that the Weibull decomposition can be applied in schools, community centers, and clinical settings where session time is limited and controlled laboratory conditions are unavailable. This extends the argument made by Bignardi et al. (2021) for tablet-based cognitive assessment to the specific domain of RT distributional analysis.

Third, the SRT/MT decomposition itself merits adoption as standard practice in reaching-based RT tasks across developmental and aging samples. Total response time in reaching paradigms conflates pre-motor cognitive processes with motor execution. In a cross-sectional sample spanning childhood through older adulthood, where motor capabilities differ substantially across age groups---young children are still refining upper-limb coordination, while older adults face biomechanical and musculoskeletal constraints---this confound is not trivial. The present results show that the Weibull parameter that dominates age-related variance *changes* depending on whether SRT or MT is analyzed. Collapsing across both phases, as is common in studies reporting only total RT, would obscure this dissociation and lead to conclusions about "processing speed" that are contaminated by motor execution differences.

### Model Limitations and Extensions

Several limitations qualify the present conclusions. First, while the posterior predictive checks indicated overall model adequacy, the variability test (*T*~sd~) identified elevated misfit rates in some groups (up to 7.1% for SRT in adolescents), suggesting that the single Weibull distribution underestimates trial-to-trial variability for a subset of participants. This is consistent with participants who produce an attentional lapse on one or more trials, generating an occasional extreme slow response that a single Weibull cannot accommodate. A contamination mixture extension---for example, a Weibull distribution for on-task trials combined with a uniform or exponential lapse component (Ratcliff & Tuerlinckx, 2002)---would provide a principled way to absorb these outlying responses without distorting the primary distributional parameters. Critically, the misfit did not concentrate in the groups central to our theoretical conclusions: the youngest children and older adults both showed 0% *T*~sd~ extreme values for SRT, meaning the model fit *best* in the groups where the dissociable-routes argument rests. The 7.1% adolescent rate should be evaluated against the expected rate under a well-specified model: with a two-tailed 10% criterion (5% in each tail), some participants will appear extreme by chance, and 7.1% is within the range expected under adequate specification. Moreover, adding a contamination component would increase model complexity in a context where trial counts are already limited (20 per participant), potentially creating identifiability problems that are a different manifestation of the data-sparsity concern. We regard the mixture extension as a worthwhile robustness check for future work with longer protocols, not as evidence that the current model is structurally inadequate for the group-level conclusions drawn here.

Second, the 20-trial protocol is shorter than the 50--100 trials typically recommended for individual-level distributional analysis via maximum likelihood (Ratcliff & Tuerlinckx, 2002). This recommendation, however, was established for non-hierarchical estimation, where each participant's parameters must be recovered from their own data alone. Under HBM, the effective information available for each individual's estimate includes both their own trials and the group-level distribution estimated from all participants in their age group. Rouder et al. (2005, Simulation Study 1) showed that hierarchical Bayesian estimation with 25 trials per participant produced lower root mean squared error than MLE with the same trial count, and comparable accuracy to MLE with substantially more trials. The bias-variance tradeoff under hierarchical estimation favors stabilized (lower-variance) estimates at the cost of some shrinkage toward the group mean. For participants near the group center, the bias is negligible and the variance reduction is substantial. For participants at the extremes, shrinkage may attenuate genuine individual differences. The net effect is to produce conservative group-level comparisons; the large effect sizes reported here (*d* = 0.83--1.65 for critical contrasts) are unlikely to be artifacts of HBM shrinkage, which would work in the opposite direction. Nevertheless, future studies should systematically investigate the trial count × HBM interaction to establish the minimum number of trials needed for reliable parameter recovery at both the individual and group levels.

Third, the cross-sectional design cannot distinguish age effects from cohort effects. The young adult sample (*n* = 1,299) was predominantly university students, while the elderly sample (*n* = 72) was community-recruited. These groups differ in education, technology familiarity, and likely in health status. Touchscreen familiarity is a specific concern: older adults less accustomed to tablet interaction may produce elevated shift values that partly reflect unfamiliarity rather than pure sensorimotor limitation. This confound is more plausible for MT, where the response requires reaching to a target on the touchscreen, than for SRT, where the response is a finger lift from a button already in sustained contact. If tablet unfamiliarity were the primary driver of the elderly *t*₀ elevation, we would expect a substantially larger effect for MT than SRT; the observed effects were comparable in magnitude (*d* = −1.38 for SRT, −1.28 for MT), consistent with a general sensorimotor slowing account rather than a device-specific confound. Nevertheless, a definitive test would require either a within-subjects control for tablet experience or a comparison with standard laboratory button-press RT. Longitudinal or accelerated longitudinal designs would further allow within-person inference and support latent growth curve modeling of individual Weibull parameters.

Fourth, the substantial group size imbalance (*n* = 56 to 1,299) raises the question of whether the HBM's partial pooling adequately serves the smaller groups. Under HBM, the group-level hyperparameters for small groups are estimated with greater uncertainty, and individual estimates within those groups are consequently shrunk more toward the group mean. This could attenuate the apparent heterogeneity in the smallest groups (adolescents, *n* = 56; older adults, *n* = 72), potentially leading to underestimation of individual differences. The practical consequence for the present analyses is that non-significant post-hoc comparisons involving these groups should be interpreted with caution, as the absence of evidence is not evidence of absence. Group-size scaling of hyperprior variances was implemented to mitigate this issue, but larger developmental samples at the age extremes remain a priority.

Fifth, the cognitive interpretations of Weibull parameters---*t*₀ as peripheral latency, λ as central processing efficiency, κ as attentional consistency---are theoretically motivated by the stage-based account (Rouder et al., 2005) and supported by the SRT--MT dissociation reported here, but they were not directly tested through experimental manipulation within this study. Temporal preparation, sustained attention, and motor demands were not independently varied. Using age differences to infer the functional meaning of parameters, and then using those meanings to explain age differences, risks circularity. We have mitigated this risk by grounding the parameter interpretations in independent evidence: the experimental selective influence results of Rouder et al. (2005), the SRT--MT crossing pattern (which does not depend on the age comparison), and consistency with the broader literature on peripheral sensorimotor slowing in aging (Bartzokis et al., 2010; Forrence et al., 2023). Nevertheless, a definitive test of selective influence would require manipulating foreperiod variability (to target κ), stimulus intensity (to target *t*₀), or cognitive load (to target λ) within a factorial developmental design and demonstrating that each manipulation affects its targeted parameter without altering the others. This is a concrete priority for subsequent studies in this research program.

### Broader Implications and Future Directions

The present findings have implications for clinical assessment, computational modeling, and lifespan research design. First, the Weibull parameter profile offers a richer descriptive vocabulary than mean RT for characterizing atypical development. Under the stage-based interpretation, a child whose slow SRT is associated with elevated *t*₀ (suggesting peripheral limitation) may warrant different clinical consideration than one whose slow SRT is associated with reduced κ (suggesting attentional inconsistency). The latter profile---specifically, inflated right-tail responses with preserved fast responses---resembles the pattern expected in ADHD, where attentional lapses are a defining feature (Kofler et al., 2013; Tamm et al., 2012). If Weibull parameters prove sensitive to ADHD-related attentional fluctuation, they could serve as distributional markers complementing traditional mean-RT screening. Similarly, preclinical cognitive decline in older adults may manifest first as changes in specific Weibull parameters before mean RT elevates to detectable levels, though this remains a hypothesis requiring longitudinal validation.

Second, the present findings raise the question of whether the Weibull shift parameter can be formally identified with the DDM's non-decision time (*T*~er~). Both parameters aim to capture the irreducible latency floor, and both are elevated in older adults. If this identification holds, the Weibull provides a decomposition of what DDM treats as a nuisance parameter: the DDM's *T*~er~ lumps together sensory encoding, motor preparation, and motor execution, while the Weibull's *t*₀ can be separately estimated for SRT and MT, and its λ and κ capture aspects of the processing that occurs above this floor. Rouder et al. (2005) proposed using the Weibull as an intermediary for fitting the DDM, and the present developmental data provide a natural test bed for this two-stage approach.

Third, the present design lacks middle-aged adults (30--65), leaving the transition between the developmental and aging parametric profiles uncharacterized. Does the shift from a λ-dominant profile (childhood) to a *t*₀-dominant profile (older adulthood)---suggesting a transition from centrally-driven to peripherally-driven slowing---occur gradually across the middle decades, or is there a relatively abrupt transition coinciding with the onset of white matter degradation in the fifth or sixth decade (Bartzokis et al., 2010)? Large-sample, web-based data collection that provides continuous age coverage across the full adult lifespan would address this question and would test whether the parametric transition mirrors the nonlinear trajectory of mean SRT established in prior work (Der & Deary, 2006; Fozard et al., 1994).

Finally, the ultimate test of the Weibull decomposition's utility is whether within-person change in specific parameters over time predicts cognitive outcomes better than mean RT change alone. If longitudinal increases in λ precede broader cognitive decline, or if within-person decreases in κ signal emerging attentional dysregulation, the Weibull decomposition would provide mechanistic specificity that aggregate speed measures cannot. Such longitudinal evidence would elevate the Weibull parameters from descriptive summaries of cross-sectional differences to prospective markers of cognitive trajectories.

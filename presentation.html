Background

Processing speed across the lifespan

The Classic U-Shaped Trajectory

  • Development: RT speeds up from childhood through young adulthood
  • Aging: RT slows from ~30 years onward
    (~0.5 ms/year; Fozard et al., 1994)
  • Kail (1991): exponential speed-up during childhood
  • Salthouse (1996): processing-speed theory of cognitive aging
Shared assumption: Processing speed is unitary — it changes in level but not in kind across the lifespan.

But is this true? Do children and older adults slow down for the same reasons?

Cognitive Factors in SRT

Even "simple" RT engages separable stages:

Stimulus → Sensory encoding → Temporal preparation → Sustained attention → Motor initiation → Response

Peripheral components

  • Sensory transduction
  • Motor initiation & execution
  • Captured by shift (t₀)

Central components

  • Temporal preparation / readiness
  • Sustained attention / consistency
  • Captured by scale (λ) & shape (κ)

The Problem

Why mean RT is not enough

Beyond the Mean

  • Intra-individual variability (IIV) captures dimensions distinct from mean RT — predicts cognitive decline (Hultsch et al., 2002)
  • Total response time in reaching paradigms conflates pre-motor cognitive processes with motor execution
  • Proportional slowing may mask qualitatively different mechanisms at each end of the lifespan
Solution: Decompose RT distributions into interpretable parameters and examine their separate developmental trajectories.

Approach

Shifted Weibull + Hierarchical Bayesian Modeling

The Shifted Weibull Distribution

Three-parameter family (Logan, 1992; Rouder et al., 2005):

ParameterSymbolInterpretation
Shiftt₀Irreducible minimum — peripheral latency
ScaleλCentral processing speed / efficiency
ShapeκAttentional consistency / tail behavior
Why Weibull? Rouder et al. (2003, 2005) developed a full HBM framework for it. Parameters map onto a processing-stage account: peripheral (t₀) vs. central (λ, κ).

Hierarchical Bayesian Estimation

Why HBM?

  • Only ~20 trials per participant — MLE is unstable with few trials
  • Partial pooling: individual estimates shrink toward group mean
  • Full posterior uncertainty propagated
  • Handles extreme group-size imbalance (n = 56 to 1,299)

Model structure

  • Individual Weibull parameters drawn from group-level hyperpriors
  • Separate hyperparameters per age group
  • MCMC sampling with Gibbs + Metropolis-Hastings
  • Group-size-scaled hyperprior variances

Tablet-Based Reaching Task

  • Touchscreen reaching paradigm
  • Hardware separation of SRT (stimulus → lift-off) and MT (lift-off → target touch)
  • 5 practice + 20 formal trials
  • Outlier removal via log-transformed Tukey's fences
Key advantage: Separating SRT from MT lets us isolate pre-motor cognitive processes from ballistic motor execution — critical for disambiguating children vs. elderly slowing.

Key Finding

Children are not small elderly

Children vs. Adults: λ-Dominant Profile

Children < 7 vs. Adults

ParameterCohen's dRank
λSRT (scale)1.58① Largest
t₀SRT (shift)1.63②
κSRT (shape)−1.01③
Children's slowing is driven by elevated scale (λ) — reflecting immature central processing and temporal preparation.

Cognitive interpretation

  • Large λ → slow, variable central processing
  • Low κ → heavy right tail → attentional lapses
  • Elevated t₀ → motor immaturity also present, but not dominant
  • Consistent with immature myelination and dopaminergic system

Elderly vs. Adults: t₀-Dominant Profile

Elderly 65+ vs. Adults

ParameterCohen's dRank
t₀SRT (shift)−1.85① Largest
λSRT (scale)−0.96②
κSRT (shape)0.65③
Elderly slowing is driven by elevated shift (t₀) — reflecting sensorimotor decline at the irreducible minimum.

Cognitive interpretation

  • Large t₀ → peripheral encoding + motor initiation slowing
  • Moderate λ increase → some central slowing, but secondary
  • κ slightly lower → mild increase in attentional inconsistency
  • Parallels DDM finding: elderly ↑ non-decision time (Ratcliff et al., 2006)

The SRT–MT Contrast as Validation

SRT (cognitive + motor)

  • Dominant parameter: λ (η² = .451)
  • Central processing differences drive age effects

MT (motor execution only)

  • Dominant parameter: t₀ (η² = .440)
  • Peripheral motor component dominates
Internal validation: The SRT/MT contrast confirms the parameter interpretations. λ captures central processing (more relevant for SRT), while t₀ captures peripheral processes (more relevant for MT).

Pooled Within-Group Correlations

  • After removing shared age trends, cross-DV correlations by parameter type remain moderate:
    t₀S–t₀M = .19, λS–λM = .22
  • κ shows negative correlations with t₀ and λ within DV (speed–consistency trade-off)
  • Parameters are sufficiently independent to justify individual-level analysis
Dedifferentiation hypothesis: Only partially supported. Young children and elderly show elevated correlations, but the pattern doesn't match the classic prediction of uniform coupling increase.

Discussion

Implications and future directions

Methodological Contributions

HBMEssential for 20-trial data
TabletDistributional analysis outside the lab
SRT/MTStandard practice for reaching tasks
  • HBM partial pooling stabilizes estimates where MLE would fail
  • Tablet-based platform enables large-scale developmental data collection
  • SRT/MT decomposition should be adopted as standard in reaching paradigms

Limitations

  • Trial count: 20 trials is below the 50–100 recommended for MLE — HBM compensates but doesn't fully resolve
  • Cross-sectional design: Cannot distinguish age from cohort effects
  • Group imbalance: Adults (n = 1,299) vs. adolescents (n = 56)
  • Missing middle age: No 30–65 age group — transition between profiles is uncharacterized
  • Parameter interpretation: Selective influence of Weibull parameters is theoretically motivated but not experimentally confirmed

Future Directions

Immediate extensions

  • Add middle-aged adults (30–65) to characterize the λ → t₀ transition
  • Experimental manipulation of foreperiod and attention to test selective influence
  • Formal Weibull–DDM parameter mapping (t₀ ↔ Ter)

Long-term goals

  • Longitudinal design: does within-person λ decline predict cognitive outcomes?
  • Clinical translation: parameter profiles as screening markers
  • Extend to Choice RT (Donders Type B) — Hick's law parameters across age

Take-Home Message

Children and older adults arrive at similarly slow reaction times via qualitatively different distributional mechanisms.

Children: λ-dominant (central processing immaturity)
Elderly: t₀-dominant (peripheral sensorimotor decline)

Mean RT conceals these distinct routes. Distributional decomposition via HBM reveals them — even with only 20 trials per participant.
